Adrienne Rich

Adrienne Rich was a poet and essayist who had many well-known publications over her decades long career some include Diving into the Wreck: Poems 1971-1972 (1973) and Of Woman Born: Motherhood as Institution and Experience (1976). Her work was famously radical as it examines the topics of feminism, politics, racism, and identity in a post-World War II America. She was awarded many honors such as the MacArthur “Genius” Award, the National Book Award, and was offered the National Medal for Arts but refused it.

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s her works were primary readings during the second wave of the Women’s Liberation Movement. They often challenged the relative role of women in society and in the home and discussed the affliction of traditional gender roles which eventually led to her separation from her husband in 1970. Rich moved to California in the 1980s with her partner Michelle Cliff. She wrote an essay titled “Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence” (1980) that declared heterosexuality a patriarchal construct that only straight men benefited from. Her work was often criticized for being angry and dark, but that is what others found inspiring.

She was a powerful and motivating voice of her time. Her career documented a metamorphosis of a compliant wife and daughter to an unequivocal social activist. Her poems left a legacy of revolution against oppressive institutions. They inspired future generations to be conscious of the world they are living in and abandon the traditions that hold them back. They shined a light on those who are often overlooked and showed off their value.
