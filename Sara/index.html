Adrienne Rich

Adrienne Rich was a poet and essayist who had many well-known publications over her decades long career some include Diving into the Wreck: Poems 1971-1972 (1973) and Of Woman Born: Motherhood as Institution and Experience (1976). Her work was famously radical as it examines the topics of feminism, politics, racism, and identity in a post-World War II America. She was awarded many honors such as the MacArthur “Genius” Award, the National Book Award, and was offered the National Medal for Arts but refused it.

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s her works were primary readings during the second wave of the Women’s Liberation Movement. They often challenged the relative role of women in society and in the home and discussed the affliction of traditional gender roles which eventually led to her separation from her husband in 1970. Rich moved to California in the 1980s with her partner Michelle Cliff. She wrote an essay titled “Compulsory Heterosexuality and Lesbian Existence” (1980) that declared heterosexuality a patriarchal construct that only straight men benefited from. Her work was often criticized for being angry and dark, but that is what others found inspiring.

She was a powerful and motivating voice of her time. Her career documented a metamorphosis of a compliant wife and daughter to an unequivocal social activist. Her poems left a legacy of gender equality and queer empowerment in California and across the nation. They inspired future generations to be conscious of the world they are living in and abandon the traditions that hold them back. They shined a light on those who are often overlooked and showed off their value.

Planetarium

“Thinking of Caroline Herschel (1750–1848) astronomer, sister of William; and others.



A woman in the shape of a monster a monster in the shape of a woman the skies are full of them



a woman ‘in the snow among the Clocks and instruments or measuring the ground with poles’ in her 98 years to discover 8 comets



she whom the moon ruled like us levitating into the night sky riding the polished lenses

Galaxies of women, there doing penance for impetuousness ribs chilled in those spaces of the mind



An eye,



‘virile, precise and absolutely certain’ from the mad webs of Uranusborg



encountering the NOVA



every impulse of light exploding



from the core as life flies out of us



Tycho whispering at last ‘Let me not seem to have lived in vain’



What we see, we see and seeing is changing



the light that shrivels a mountain and leaves a man alive



Heartbeat of the pulsar heart sweating through my body



The radio impulse pouring in from Taurus

I am bombarded yet I stand



I have been standing all my life in the direct path of a battery of signals the most accurately transmitted most untranslatable language in the universe I am a galactic cloud so deep so invo- luted that a light wave could take 15 years to travel through me And has taken I am an instrument in the shape of a woman trying to translate pulsations into images for the relief of the body and the reconstruction of the mind.”

-Adrienne Rich

In this poem, Rich criticizes and comments on the expectations that society places on women. She does what is surprising when she honors women’s discoveries in astronomy and science and when referring to women in an indelicate and sordid manner. She highlights achievements that fall beyond what was anticipated from women. The last section of the poem explains the discomfort Rich experienced being a woman and then alludes to disembodiment and being unattached from womanhood. The format of the poem is a vessel that also holds meaning. It begins somewhat unorganized and surprising. It does not follow any usual layout. Then it conforms to the standard couplets and then reverts back to rejecting common format by finishing the poem in a large block of text. The poem itself is a metaphor for the experience of a woman.
