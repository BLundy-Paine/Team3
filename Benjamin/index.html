George Sterling "King of Bohemia"

Early Life and San Francisco 2
  • Sterling was born in Sag Harbor, NY in 1869, he moved to Oakland in 1890 to avoid the priesthood. He began to write poetry on ferry trips to work, which he shared with friends such as Jack London and Mary Austin. Sterling and his friends enjoyed the company of high profile men and women at the Bohemian Club in San Francisco, they would drink, put on plays, and spend wild nights in the Bohemian Grove.
Carmel 3
  • In 1905 SF's "King of Bohemia" left to start his own bohemian colony and get away from the dunkard lifestyle of San Francisco, Sterling and his wife Carrie Rand moved to a house near Carmel beach. Sterling's friends would visit, bringing the artistic and non-traditional subculture of Bohemianism to Carmel. Previously a rather simple and quiet town, Sterling's fantastical poetry, Bohemianism, and artists friends shaped Carmel into a popular tourists destination.
Controversy and Suicides 4
  • Unfortunately Bohemianism wasn't that positive of a lifestyle, the macabre was a common theme in poetry and conversation. Writer Nora May French was the first in a string of Bohemian suicides, drinking cyanide in Sterling's home in 1907. Soon a suicide pact was formed and cyanide was acquired for when "life got too hard". Among others, Sterling's wife Carrie took her cyanide in 1918, and Sterling himself in 1926.

The Last Days 10

(1914)

The russet leaves of the sycamore
Lie at last on the valley floor—
By the autumn wind swept to and fro
Like ghosts in a tale of long ago.
Shallow and dear the Carmel glides
Where the willows droop on its vine-walled sides.

The bracken-rust is red on the hill;
The pines stand brooding, somber and still;
Grey are the cliffs, and the waters grey,
Where the seagulls dip to the sea-born spray.
Sad November, lady of rain,
Sends the goose-wedge over again.

Wilder now, for the verdure’s birth,
Falls the sunlight over the earth;
Killdees call from the fields where now
The banding blackbirds follow the plow;
Rustling poplar and brittle weed
Whisper low to the river-reed.

Days departing linger and sigh:
Stars come soon to the quiet sky;
Buried voices, intimate, strange,
Cry to body and soul of Change;
Beauty, eternal fugitive,
Seeks the home that we cannot give.

Commentary

This four stanza poem originally titled "By Carmel Mission", depicts Mission and its surroundings during autumn. Sterling illustrates both the physical and emotional aspects of his senses, fantastical yet genuine, explicating the artistry the Bohemian era is so well known for. His multiple metaphors comparing nature to the humanistic personify the things which may seem distant, reminding us of our similarities.


In the last stanza, Sterling notes the duality intrinsic to life, the familiarity and liveliness of the day contrasted by the mystery and stillness of the night. By capitalizing "Change", he emphasizes the crucial and yet impossible goal of the night; to disrobe itself of mystery and 'turn' into the day. Sterling calls this "the home that we cannot give"; the day is not the night changed, it is a new being, an "eternal fugitive" forever chased.


This poem can be hard to understand with its antiquated language, here are some useful definitions:

  • Carmel glides — A "glide" is a length of calm, shallow, smoothly flowing water, Sterling is referring to the Carmel River which flows by the Mission
  • Bracken -rust — A "bracken" is a tall fern and "rust" refers to a reddish-brown plant fungi that commonly grows of ferns
  • Goose-wedge — The V-shaped formation of which geese fly in
  • Verdure — The green color of growing plants
  • Killdees — Most likely an improper pluralization referring to the common shorebird "Killdeer" named for its onomatopoeic call
